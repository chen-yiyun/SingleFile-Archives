    Undercurrents

    ohlooktheresabee

    Summary:

    Sherlock thought John knew him better than anyone - that he understood the parts of him others never cared to. But after an ill-conceived attempt by Greg and John to set him up with someone, Sherlock is left feeling betrayed, misunderstood, and deeply hurt. Old wounds resurface, and the trust Sherlock has in John fractures under the weight of assumptions. As emotions spiral out of control, Sherlock must confront his fear of rejection, while John struggles to repair the damage he didn’t realize he’d caused. After quite the shouting match, they finally realize that all they ever wanted was each other.

    Work Text:

    The bell above the restaurant door chimed softly as Sherlock entered. The warmth of the room, the low murmur of conversation, and the faint scent of polished wood and wine filled the air. It wasn’t unpleasant, but it already felt tedious. Social gatherings were a necessity he tolerated at best. Tonight was meant to be a brief celebration of the hard case they had just solved—something John had insisted on. He wasn’t entirely sure why he had agreed. He scanned the room with a practiced eye, immediately locating John and Lestrade seated at a table toward the back. 

    Sherlock approached, his mind already cataloguing details: John looked tired. No, more than tired—there was something heavy about him today. His smile didn’t reach his eyes, and though he was making an effort to appear engaged, the tension in his jaw gave him away. Lestrade, by contrast, looked uncharacteristically upbeat, his eyes bright and mouth curled in an easy grin. Between them sat a man Sherlock didn’t recognize - mid-thirties, dark hair styled meticulously, a well-fitted blazer that suggested both affluence and an eye for design.

    “Sherlock!” Lestrade called out, waving him over as though the room were too large for him to notice them otherwise. “Glad you made it!”

    Sherlock took the empty seat, nodding once in greeting but keeping his eyes on the stranger.

    “This is Claude,” Lestrade said, gesturing toward the man. “He’s an old friend of mine from university - just moved here from Paris.”

    Claude extended a hand with a polite smile. “Nice to meet you, Sherlock. Greg has told me quite a bit about you.”

    Sherlock shook his hand briefly, his mind already working. Calloused fingertips but clean nails - likely works with materials but not in a rough trade. The slight sheen on his collar suggested a recent steam press, and the faint scent of coffee clung to him - not from the restaurant but from somewhere else, likely an office.

    “You are an architect,” Sherlock said in French, noting the flicker of surprise on Claude’s face.

    “Yes,” Claude replied in English, his smile widening. “Very impressive. Greg said you had quite the talent for… observation. He didn’t mention languages.”

    Sherlock didn’t respond, instead glancing at John, who seemed unusually quiet. His usual energy was absent, replaced by something more subdued. Beneath the forced smile and polite nods, there was a heaviness to him - a sadness he was clearly trying to hide.

    Lestrade, on the other hand, appeared almost too cheerful, as though trying to keep the mood light. Sherlock turned back to Claude, who was still smiling politely but seemed slightly nervous, perhaps trying to make a good impression. The whole scene felt… off.

    They talked - well, Lestrade talked mostly - about nothing in particular: how difficult the case had been, how they should celebrate more often, how nice it was to have someone new in town. Sherlock participated minimally, offering the occasional nod or monosyllabic response. His social energy was draining rapidly, and he was already calculating how soon he could leave without appearing rude.

    It wasn’t until Lestrade made an offhand comment - “Claude’s still settling in. Thought he might like to meet some new people” - that something clicked in Sherlock’s mind. He glanced at John, whose forced cheer had faltered momentarily, then at Claude, who was now looking at him expectantly.

    Of course. A setup.

    The realization hit with a quiet but undeniable force. They had brought him here under the guise of celebration but had, in truth, arranged a meeting designed to introduce him to a potential romantic partner.

    Sherlock felt a sudden, sharp pang in his chest. He wasn’t sure what hurt more - the setup itself or the fact that John, of all people, had gone along with it. After everything, after all the times Sherlock had explicitly said he wasn’t interested in dating, wasn’t interested in romance, here they were, trying to push it on him anyway. He had told him before - many times - that he wasn’t interested in romance. He didn’t do relationships. He didn’t want them. And yet, here he was, ignoring Sherlock’s words, dismissing his boundaries as though they didn’t matter.

    John, his best friend John was doing this. John, who Sherlock secretly - No. Don’t think about that.

    Sherlock kept his expression carefully neutral, unwilling to let the sting show. But inside, a quiet voice whispered: He doesn’t know you at all, does he? After all these years, he still doesn’t understand you.

    Claude said something Sherlock didn’t catch, his voice drowned out by the rush of blood in Sherlock’s ears. When he realized everyone was looking at him, he forced a polite smile, though it felt like a mask slipping out of place.

    “Sorry,” Sherlock said abruptly, his voice quieter than usual. “I think I’ve lost track of the conversation.”

    Claude tilted his head slightly, concern flickering in his eyes. “Are you all right?” he asked, switching to French. “You seem… uncomfortable.”

    Sherlock blinked, caught off guard by the sudden change in language. He hesitated for a moment before replying, his voice low and carefully measured. “I’m sorry, but I’m aromantic and asexual. I don’t… do relationships. I believe there’s been a misunderstanding.”

    “Ah, I see. Did your friends not know this?”

    “I… I thought they knew. I’ve told them before,” Sherlock said, immediately regretting saying so much as he tried to calm himself down.

    Claude’s eyes widened slightly as he processed the information. Then, to Sherlock’s surprise, his expression darkened—not with offense, but with annoyance. Turning toward Lestrade and John, Claude launched into a rapid stream of French, his tone sharp and accusing. Though Sherlock didn’t catch every word, he understood enough. Claude was telling them off—angrily, emphatically, defending Sherlock in a way that left him feeling strangely mortified.

    “You should have told me,” Claude said in English, clearly for their benefit. “This was inappropriate.”

    John opened his mouth, perhaps to explain or protest, but Claude didn’t give him the chance.

    “Sherlock made it clear he isn’t interested in this sort of thing, didn’t he?” Claude continued. “You should respect that.”

    Sherlock’s face flushed with embarrassment. He didn’t want this - didn’t want to be defended like he was helpless, didn’t want attention drawn to the very thing that made him feel so… other. He stood abruptly, the chair scraping loudly against the floor.

    “I need to go,” he said shortly, avoiding their eyes and grabbing his coat.

    “Sherlock - ” John began, but Sherlock didn’t stop. He made his way out of the restaurant quickly, stepping into the cold night air and taking a deep breath.

    The hurt lingered, sharp and unrelenting. As he walked home, he tried to think about anything else, didn’t want to think about why it hurt so much - why John’s involvement in this farce felt like such a betrayal. All he knew was that, once again, people had tried to force him into a mould he didn’t fit, and it left him feeling more isolated than ever.

    Sherlock woke with a start, his heart racing, the remnants of the dream lingering like a faint echo. The grey morning light seeped through the curtains, casting the room in a dim, muted glow. He sat up slowly, pressing the heels of his hands against his eyes, trying to push away the unsettling images still fresh in his mind.

    The courtroom had been absurd - a warped version of reality, but it didn’t make the feeling any less real. John had stood as the judge, his expression cold, distant, implacable. Behind him, a crowd of faceless figures had jeered and laughed, pointing fingers as if Sherlock were on trial for the simple act of being himself. And John… John had rendered judgment without hesitation.

    Sherlock exhaled slowly, dragging himself out of bed. The dream was ridiculous, of course, but it left an uncomfortable residue, a lingering sense that something was fundamentally wrong. He had felt it from the moment he’d walked out of the restaurant the previous night, but now it pressed on him more heavily, more insistently.

    He moved through the flat in silence, deliberately avoiding John’s presence. He could hear the soft creak of floorboards below, the familiar sounds of John moving about in the kitchen, making tea, tidying up. It should have been comforting, but today it felt… wrong.

    Everything felt wrong.

    Sherlock stayed in his room, pacing restlessly, mind racing in circles he couldn’t seem to break free from. He tried to read, tried to focus on anything that might occupy his thoughts, but nothing worked. Every time he tried to distract himself, his mind dragged him back to the same place, the same thought: He doesn’t understand you. He never did.

    He had always thought John was different. That, unlike others, John didn’t expect him to change. That he didn’t see Sherlock’s lack of interest in relationships or romance as a flaw, but simply as another part of who he was. But last night had shattered that illusion.

    You thought he accepted you, the voice in his head whispered, relentless and cruel. You thought you were enough. But you were wrong. He wants something else. Something normal.

    The idea settled heavily in his chest, an uncomfortable pressure he couldn’t shake. Sherlock had allowed himself to believe that their partnership was enough - that they had reached an understanding. He had even begun to hope, foolishly, that things between them were growing deeper, closer. Not in the way others might define it, but in a way that made sense for them.

    And yet, here they were. John had gone along with the setup, as though Sherlock’s words over the years had meant nothing. As though everything they had built was somehow lacking - something in need of fixing, of improvement.

    The thought made his skin feel tight, his breath shallow. He knew it shouldn’t matter. Objectively, it had been a simple misunderstanding, a misguided attempt at kindness. Lestrade and John had likely meant well. But the intention didn’t change how it felt - the sharp sense of dismissal, of being unseen. Again.

    He replayed the conversation in the restaurant over and over, picking apart every detail, every expression. He should have seen it sooner, should have deduced the purpose of the evening before it had blindsided him. Instead, he had been caught off guard, exposed in a way that made him feel raw, vulnerable.

    You should have known. You’re supposed to see things coming. But you didn’t. And now look at you - hiding in your own flat, unable to face him because you’re too…

    Sherlock didn’t finish the thought. He didn’t want to name the feeling, didn’t want to acknowledge just how much this had affected him. He paced the room again, his footsteps echoing softly against the wooden floor. The familiar rhythm usually helped to steady his mind, but today it did little more than emphasize the growing disquiet.

    He hated this. Hated the way his mind refused to let go, the way the tension in his chest refused to ease. He had always prided himself on being above such things - above emotion, above the petty insecurities that plagued others. But now, he couldn’t seem to escape it.

    Time passed in a blur of restless pacing and half-formed thoughts. The hunger in his stomach grew steadily more insistent, but the idea of facing John made the prospect of leaving his room unbearable. He stayed put, ignoring the gnawing ache, focusing instead on keeping his composure, on maintaining the illusion of control.

    Control. That was what this was about, wasn’t it? Last night had robbed him of control - over the situation, over his emotions, over the carefully constructed persona he presented to the world. He hadn’t been able to hide how it affected him, hadn’t been able to shield himself from the sting of it. And now, he was left grappling with something he didn’t want to acknowledge: that it mattered.

    It mattered because it was John. Because Sherlock had believed, perhaps foolishly, that John saw him as something more than an anomaly to be fixed. He had thought they had reached a place where understanding didn’t need to be spoken, where acceptance was simply a given. But now, he wasn’t so sure.

    The sound of footsteps outside his door pulled him from his thoughts. He froze, heart pounding, listening as John paused briefly, as though considering knocking. Sherlock held his breath, waiting. But after a moment, the footsteps continued, fading away as John moved down the hall.

    Sherlock exhaled slowly, tension easing slightly from his shoulders. He wasn’t ready to face him. Not yet. He needed time - to think, to sort through the mess of thoughts and emotions swirling in his mind.

    For now, all he could do was retreat further into the quiet of his room, hoping that, eventually, he would find a way to put the pieces back together.

    The following morning, Sherlock awoke from another unpleasant dream. It had been strange, unsettling in its vagueness. He was in a vast, featureless expanse - a pale grey void stretching endlessly in all directions. The ground beneath his feet had been unsteady, shifting unpredictably with each step, as though it couldn’t quite decide whether to hold his weight or let him fall.

    In the distance, he had seen familiar shapes - people, buildings, places he knew - but they flickered in and out of existence like mirages, never solid enough to approach. He had tried walking toward them, but no matter how far he moved, they remained distant, unreachable.

    There had been a voice, too - John’s voice. It had called out to him, but the words had been indistinct, warped by the void until they were little more than echoes of something just out of reach. Every time he tried to respond, his voice caught in his throat, and the ground beneath him trembled, threatening to give way entirely.

    The worst part had been the feeling - not fear exactly, but a deep, pervasive sense of isolation, as though he existed in a world parallel to everyone else, forever just a step removed from connection. He had awoken just as the ground beneath him had started to crumble, the void collapsing inward, leaving him suspended in nothingness.

    Now, in the quiet stillness of the flat, the dream lingered at the edges of his mind, like a shadow he couldn’t quite shake. He sat up slowly, his breathing steady but shallow, as though part of him hadn’t yet left that shifting, unstable expanse. He forced himself out of bed, moving mechanically as he dressed. The flat felt too small, too stifling, and he knew he needed to get out. 

    The thought of heading to Barts presented itself as a lifeline. The sterile halls, the quiet hum of equipment, the predictable logic of experiments - it was the perfect distraction. He would spend the day there, running tests, drowning out everything else in the comforting precision of science. All he had to do was get through the living room and out the door without engaging in conversation.

    Simple enough.

    He drew in a slow breath before opening his door, carefully schooling his expression into one of nonchalance. The key was to act normal - calm, detached, indifferent.

    Just breeze through. Don’t give him a chance to start anything.

    He stepped out into the living room with as much casual detachment as he could muster. John was exactly where he expected - seated in his chair, tea in hand, eyes flicking up the moment Sherlock entered. The tension in the air was immediate, tangible.

    Sherlock kept moving, aiming straight for the door where his coat and scarf hung. His fingers wrapped around the coat as he pulled it off the hook, already calculating how fast he could leave.

    “Sherlock, hold on,” John said, setting his mug down with a quiet clink and getting to his feet.

    Sherlock didn’t respond, methodically slipping into his coat and adjusting the scarf. He could feel John’s eyes on him, could hear the deliberate steps as he approached.

    “I need to say something,” John continued. “Look, I know the other night was uncomfortable, but I wasn’t trying to… I didn’t plan it. Greg did, and I should’ve stopped him once I realized what he was doing. I didn’t. That’s on me. I’m sorry.”

    Sherlock’s fingers faltered briefly before tightening on the scarf. His pulse quickened, and the tightness in his chest returned. He didn’t want to do this - didn’t want to hear John’s half-explanations and apologies when the damage was already done. He just needed to leave.

    “It’s fine,” Sherlock said shortly, his voice clipped. He turned toward the door, reaching for the handle.

    “Come on, Sherlock,” John said, his voice sharper now, frustration evident. “Don’t just brush me off. I’m trying to talk to you.”

    “I really have to go,” Sherlock said, quieter but more urgent. He tugged at the door, but John moved to block him, his stance firm.

    “Can you stop for one second?” John snapped, exasperated. “I’m trying to explain! I didn’t mean for it to turn out like that. I didn’t mean to make you uncomfortable, all right? But blowing past me like this isn’t helping.”

    Sherlock’s breath hitched, the pressure in his chest threatening to boil over. He couldn’t do this. He couldn’t stand here and listen to John try to explain away something that had shaken the very foundation of the trust between them. He needed to leave before he completely lost control.

    “I said it’s fine!” he barked, shoving past John with more force than he intended. The door swung open, and Sherlock stepped out onto the landing, cold air rushing over him.

    But he didn’t get far.

    Two steps down the narrow staircase, and the world started spinning. His chest tightened further, each breath coming shorter and faster, as though the air had suddenly turned too thin. His vision blurred, the edges darkening as a sense of overwhelming dread gripped him.

    Not here. Not now. Keep moving.

    He tried to force himself forward, but his legs felt unsteady, his hand gripping the railing to keep from collapsing. The panic clawed at him, fierce and unrelenting, and no matter how hard he tried, he couldn’t seem to get enough air.

    “Sherlock!” John’s voice cut through the haze, but it only made things worse. He was the reason this was happening - the reason Sherlock couldn’t breathe, couldn’t think. He tried to wave John off, tried to take another step, but his body wouldn’t cooperate.

    “Get… away,” Sherlock choked out, his voice strained and barely audible. He didn’t want John’s help. He didn’t want John anywhere near him.

    But John wasn’t moving. Instead, he grabbed Sherlock’s arm, steadying him with a grip that was firm but not forceful. “Hey, hey, stop,” John said, his tone shifting - no longer irate, now calm and focused, the way it always was when he slipped into doctor mode. “You’re having a panic attack. Just breathe, all right? In through your nose, out through your mouth. Slowly.”

    Sherlock tried to pull away, but his strength was fading fast, and the dizziness made it impossible to coordinate his movements. John’s grip didn’t loosen. “Come on, you know how to do this,” John said, his voice low and steady. “In through your nose, out through your mouth. Focus on me.”

    Sherlock didn’t want to focus on him. John was the source of the chaos in his mind, the reason he was standing here, gasping for air like a drowning man. But there was no other option - no escape, no distraction. Just John’s voice, calm and insistent.

    He tried to mimic John’s breathing, dragging air in through his nose and releasing it slowly through his mouth. It was uneven, shaky, but gradually, the tightness in his chest began to ease. The fog in his mind lifted just enough for him to regain some semblance of control.

    After a few more breaths, he managed to straighten slightly, shrugging off John’s grip with trembling hands. “I’m fine,” he muttered, though the words felt hollow even to him.

    John didn’t look convinced, but he didn’t press the issue. “You’re not fine, but all right,” he said, stepping back just enough to give Sherlock space. “Do you want to sit down, or are you going to keep pretending you’re fine and head out anyway?”

    Sherlock didn’t answer immediately. His mind was still racing, but the immediate panic had subsided, leaving him drained and unsteady. He didn’t know what to say - what he could say that wouldn’t make things worse.

    “I need to go,” he said finally, his voice quieter now but no less determined.

    John gave him a long, searching look before nodding once. “All right. But if you need to talk - whenever you’re ready - you know where I am.”

    Sherlock didn’t respond. Without another word, he turned and descended the rest of the stairs, stepping out onto the street. The cold air hit him again, bracing and sharp, but it did little to clear the weight pressing on his mind.

    As he walked away, he tried to focus on the familiar rhythm of his footsteps, tried to lose himself in the predictable pattern of sound and motion. But no matter how hard he tried, John’s words circled in his mind like an unwelcome echo.

    The sterile hum of the lab at Barts should have been calming, but it only added to the restless tension in Sherlock’s mind. He moved methodically from one task to the next, logging data, checking results, and recalibrating instruments. He worked in silence, focusing entirely on the precision of his movements, as though perfecting each step would stop the thoughts swirling inside him.

    It didn’t. The knot in his chest remained, tight and unyielding, a reminder of everything he didn’t want to think about - John’s words, the setup, the feeling of being dismissed.

    He barely registered the sound of the door opening until Molly’s voice broke the quiet. “Hey, Sherlock.”

    He stiffened slightly but didn’t look up. Molly wasn’t a threat, but she wasn’t what he wanted right now either. He didn’t respond, hoping she might take the hint and leave.

    She didn’t. Instead, he heard the sound of her setting something down on a nearby counter, followed by the quiet rustle of her bag. “I, um… thought you might be here and… well, you know, here you are.”

    Sherlock kept his eyes fixed on the microscope, adjusting the slide with deliberate precision. He didn’t need to look to know Molly was hovering nearby, awkwardly shifting her weight as she tried to decide what to say next.

    Several minutes passed in silence, during which she made no further attempts to engage him. Instead, she moved around the lab quietly, sorting through samples and files. Her presence was unobtrusive, patient, undemanding. She didn’t push for conversation, didn’t interrupt his work, but she didn’t leave either.

    Eventually, his stomach growled audibly, breaking the stillness. Sherlock ignored it, focusing instead on rechecking a set of data, but Molly wasn’t one to overlook such things.

    Without a word, she pulled a sandwich from her bag and placed it gently on the table beside him. “It’s, um, egg and cress. If you don’t like it, that’s okay. You can just… ignore it.” Her voice trailed off awkwardly, but she didn’t take the sandwich away.

    Sherlock didn’t touch it, but he didn’t push it away either. He continued working, hoping that silence would discourage further conversation.

    But Molly didn’t leave. She remained nearby, working quietly on her own tasks, occasionally glancing at him but never speaking unless it was to ask a simple, routine question - nothing personal, nothing intrusive. Hours passed this way, with Molly’s quiet presence slowly lowering the walls he had erected around himself.

    At first, he found it irritating - the way she lingered without demanding anything from him, her awkward yet genuine attempts to be supportive. But as the minutes stretched into hours, the irritation faded, replaced by something else. Her quiet persistence, her refusal to leave even when he gave her nothing in return, was disarming in a way he never expected.

    Eventually, it was Molly who broke the silence, though she did so cautiously, as though unsure whether it was the right time. “You, uh, seem a bit… tense today,” she said, her voice hesitant but kind. “Not that you aren’t, um, normally tense. Just, you know, more tense.”

    Sherlock didn’t respond immediately. He adjusted the focus on the microscope, jaw tightening slightly. “I’m fine.”

    “Right,” Molly said quickly. “Of course. But if you weren’t fine, you know… you could talk to me. If you wanted. I mean, no pressure or anything. Just… putting it out there.”

    More silence followed. Molly fidgeted slightly but didn’t press further. She returned to her work, sorting through files with quiet efficiency, but Sherlock could feel her occasional glances, could sense her concern lingering in the air.

    Several more minutes passed before he spoke, his voice flat and emotionless. “John and Greg arranged something. A setup.”

    Molly froze briefly, then continued sorting, though her movements were slower now. “Oh. Uh… a setup? Like, you mean… a date?”

    “Yes.” Sherlock’s tone was clipped, each word precise and cold. “Despite knowing I wasn’t interested. Despite everything I’ve said.”

    Molly set the files down, turning slightly toward him but keeping her distance. “Oh. That’s… um, not great, is it? I mean, that sounds… yeah. Not great. For you, I mean.”

    Sherlock didn’t respond, but his fingers stilled briefly on the table before resuming their work. He knew Molly meant well, but her awkward attempts to empathize only highlighted how little most people understood about him.

    “They probably thought they were, um, helping,” Molly added, her voice quiet and tentative. “Not that it was the right thing to do! Just, you know, sometimes people think they’re being nice when they’re… really not.”

    “It’s not about whether they thought they were helping,” Sherlock said tightly, his tone sharper now. “It’s about the fact that they ignored something fundamental. I trusted John to understand. I thought he accepted that I don’t want… that.”

    Molly nodded slowly, biting her lip as she searched for a response. “Yeah. I can see why you’d feel… well, kind of betrayed, I guess. You thought he… got it, and now it feels like maybe he didn’t.”

    Sherlock’s fingers curled slightly around the edge of the table. “It’s not about feelings. It’s about trust. He dismissed something I’ve made explicitly clear.” He exhaled slowly, forcing his voice back into a more measured tone. “And now… I don’t know.”

    Molly shifted on her feet, looking uncomfortable but unwilling to let the conversation drop. “Maybe he didn’t really get it before. But… maybe he does now? And, uh, if you told him how much it… shook you, maybe he’d… I don’t know, understand better?”

    The thought made Sherlock’s skin crawl. The idea of explaining something so personal, so deeply rooted, felt unbearable. He wasn’t good at that. He never had been. He didn’t want to have to explain himself, didn’t want to make himself vulnerable in that way.

    But Molly, awkward and fumbling though she was, wasn’t wrong.

    “I know that’s not… easy,” Molly said gently, her voice soft but earnest. “But… John cares about you, Sherlock. And if he didn’t realize before how much this hurt you, maybe he deserves a chance to… fix things. If you’re ready. No rush or anything, just… when you are.”

    Sherlock didn’t respond immediately. He turned back to the microscope, pretending to focus on the slide, but his mind wasn’t on the work anymore. After a long pause, he glanced at her. “Thank you,” he said quietly, the words awkward but genuine.

    Molly smiled, clearly a little unsure of what to do with the gratitude. “Anytime. And, um, don’t forget the sandwich. It’s probably a bit squashed now, but… still edible.”

    Sherlock gave the sandwich another brief glance before returning to his work.

    The walk back to 221B felt like an endless loop of rising tension and fraying nerves. Sherlock moved quickly, his coat flaring behind him in the bitter wind, but the cold did little to temper the heat building in his chest. His mind was spinning scenarios, each one worse than the last, and by the time he reached the door, his mind was teetering on the edge of chaos.

    This wasn’t just anger. It was hurt, raw and unfiltered, tangled with fear he didn’t want to name. He couldn’t shake the feeling that something between him and John had fundamentally changed—that this might be the moment everything fell apart.

    He hesitated briefly at the door, a flicker of uncertainty crossing his mind, but he pushed it aside. There was no going back now. He needed to speak. John needed to understand.

    John was in the kitchen, leaning against the counter. He looked up as Sherlock entered, his expression a mixture of concern and guarded wariness. “You’re back,” he said carefully. “You okay?”

    Sherlock didn’t answer. He shrugged off his coat with stiff, precise movements, hanging it by the door before crossing the room in a series of sharp, restless steps. His mind raced, words forming and reforming before he finally spoke.

    “We need to talk,” he said curtly, his voice tight.

    John frowned slightly, stepping forward. “Okay. About the other night?”

    “Yes, about the other night,” Sherlock snapped, his tone clipped. He started pacing, unable to keep still as the tension within him grew. “What you and Lestrade did - it wasn’t just awkward or uncomfortable. It was dismissive.”

    John raised an eyebrow, clearly not following. “Dismissive? How?”

    “You ignored everything I’ve ever told you!” Sherlock turned sharply to face him, his voice rising. “I trusted you, John. Trusted you to understand who I am, what I want. And instead, you went along with some ridiculous plan to - what? Fix me? Change me?”

    John’s expression hardened, his frustration beginning to surface. “That’s not fair. I didn’t even know what Greg was planning until we were already there.”

    “And you didn’t stop it,” Sherlock said, pacing again, his steps quick and agitated. “You could have stopped it. You could have said no, but you didn’t. Instead, you sat there and watched while I was - ” He broke off, his breath hitching slightly. “You watched while I was humiliated.”

    John’s jaw clenched. “Humiliated? It wasn’t like that. You’re blowing this way out of proportion. It wasn’t some grand betrayal, Sherlock. It was Greg being an idiot, and I didn’t stop it because I didn’t think it was that big of a deal.”

    Sherlock stopped dead in his tracks, feeling as though he had been struck. The knot in his chest tightened painfully, his breathing quickening. “You didn’t think it was a big deal?” He was hardly able to think, he was so angry. “What exactly did you and Lestrade think was going to happen?” His voice rose with each word, his frustration barely contained. “Did you think I’d laugh it off? Did you think it would be funny?”

    John’s brow furrowed. “No, of course not - ”

    “Then what?” Sherlock cut in, turning sharply to face him. “Did you think it would be flattering? That I’d be grateful for your attempt at matchmaking?”

    “It wasn’t like that,” John said defensively. “Greg thought it might be nice. I told you, I didn’t even know what he was planning until you were already there!”

    Sherlock’s eyes narrowed, the tight knot in his chest twisting painfully. “And you think that makes it better?” he asked bitterly. “You think that excuses it? You sat there, John. You sat right there and let it happen.”

    John’s frustration flared. “It was only a meal, Sherlock! Greg was being an idiot, but it didn’t go anywhere, and I wouldn’t have let it. You’re acting like it was some kind of personal attack.”

    John still wasn’t understanding. Sherlock resumed pacing, his steps quicker now, more erratic, knowing what he had to say to get the message through but hating the thought of saying it. “Do you have any idea what could have happened if Claude hadn’t been… kind? If he hadn’t been understanding?”

    John frowned, the shift in Sherlock’s tone catching him off guard. “What do you mean?”

    “I mean people like me don’t always get lucky, John,” Sherlock said, his voice rising again, trembling with barely restrained emotion. “I mean I’ve been mocked, humiliated, assaulted because I wouldn’t sleep with people. I’ve had people lash out, call me broken, try to force me to…” He broke off, his breath hitching slightly, forcing himself to keep going. “People don’t take rejection well. They don’t take difference well.”

    John’s face paled, the weight of Sherlock’s words apparently finally sinking in. He opened his mouth to speak, but nothing came out.

    “You didn’t think about that, did you?” Sherlock continued, pacing again. “You didn’t think that maybe there are reasons I don’t want to be put in those situations. Reasons I avoid… all of it.” His hands clenched into fists at his sides, trembling. “You didn’t think, because you assumed it didn’t matter. That I didn’t matter.”

    John took a step forward, his expression shifting from defensive to horrified. “Sherlock, I didn’t know. I didn’t realize - ”

    “No, you didn’t,” Sherlock interrupted, his voice cracking slightly and he hated it. He stopped pacing abruptly, turning to face John while he still could. “Because you’re just like everyone else. You think you know better. You think you can decide for me what I should want, what I should feel.”

    “That’s not true,” John said, but his voice lacked its earlier certainty. “I’ve never tried to change you.”

    “No? Then what was that?” Sherlock’s voice rose again, out of his control, bitter and accusatory. “An accident? A joke? An attempt to make me normal?”

    John flinched, visibly shaken. “You are normal, Sherlock. There’s nothing wrong with you.”

    Sherlock froze, his breath catching in his throat. The words caught him off guard, cutting through the anger like a blade. For a moment, the room was silent, the tension hanging thick in the air.

    John exhaled slowly, his frustration giving way to something softer, more genuine. “You’re normal - whatever that word even means. And I’m sorry I didn’t realize how much that situation would hurt you. I didn’t think, because I didn’t understand. But I do now. And I’m not trying to change you - I never have.”

    Sherlock didn’t move, didn’t speak. His mind was racing, emotions crashing over him too quickly to process. He felt exposed, vulnerable in a way he couldn't stand, and the knot of tension in his chest tightened again.

    “I trusted you,” he said quietly, his voice trembling with emotion no matter how hard he tried to stop it. “I trusted you, and you made me feel like…” He trailed off, swallowing hard. “Like I’m not enough.”

    John took a cautious step forward, his voice low and steady. “You are enough, Sherlock. You’ve always been enough. I just… I didn’t understand before. But I do now.”

    Sherlock’s thoughts spiralled, each one sharper and heavier than the last, looping back to the same unbearable conclusion - John didn’t understand, no matter what he said. Not then. Maybe not ever. The words John had just spoken barely registered, their weight failing to reach the part of Sherlock that still ached, raw and exposed.

    He felt trapped. Trapped in the flat, trapped in his own mind, trapped by the fear that this - whatever this was - would end the same way it always did, no matter what conclusions John was reaching. People who didn’t understand eventually grew tired, eventually gave up. They might try, they might even mean well, but it never lasted. It always ended with distance, rejection, and being left behind.

    The echoes of past experiences crowded his mind: moments when he had dared to let people in, only for them to turn away when they realized he didn’t fit their expectations. People who had made him feel like he was an unsolvable puzzle, something defective because he couldn’t give them what they wanted.

    He couldn’t go through that again. Not with John. Especially not with John.

    The tension in his chest tightened painfully. He felt exposed. He couldn’t think, couldn’t stay here any longer, couldn’t bear the risk of hearing more well-meaning reassurances that did nothing to quell the storm inside him. Sherlock turned abruptly, heading for the door. He needed air, distance, anything to escape.

    “Wait - where are you going?” John asked, sounding alarmed.

    “Out,” Sherlock said shortly, reaching for the doorknob.

    But before he could leave, John’s voice stopped him.

    “I love you!”

    The words cut through the tension like a knife, freezing Sherlock in place. His hand tightened on the doorknob, his mind reeling as he tried to process what he had just heard. He slowly turned back to face John, whose face had flushed bright red.

    “You… what?”

    Now it was John who looked close to panicking. “I didn’t mean to shout it like that,” John said, his face still flushed. He rubbed the back of his neck awkwardly, his eyes darting to the door and back to Sherlock. “But it’s true. I love you. I didn’t realize how badly I’d messed up until you started talking, and I - ” He stopped, exhaling shakily.

    Sherlock stared at him, still frozen in place. The anger that had fueled him moments ago was fading rapidly, replaced by confusion and something far more disorienting. “You… love me?”

    John nodded, swallowing hard. “Yeah. I do. And that’s why… why it was all such a mess, okay?” His words came out rushed, jumbled together as though he was trying to force them out before his courage failed him entirely. “When Greg brought Claude, I was… miserable. I thought you’d like him, that you’d end up with him, and I just - ” He broke off again, running a hand through his hair, visibly agitated. “I thought you’d never want to be with me. Not… not like that.”

    Sherlock frowned slightly, trying to make sense of John’s words. “You thought I’d… want to be with Claude?”

    “Yes!” John said, his voice rising slightly in frustration, though it wasn’t directed at Sherlock. “I thought you’d like him because he’s smart, good-looking, and normal, and I’m just… me. And then I thought even if you did want something with me, it wouldn’t work because I - ” He stopped, his breath hitching slightly. “I’m not attracted to men. Not like that. But none of that matters because I just… I want you, Sherlock. However that looks, whatever it means. I just want us to be together.”

    John’s breathing was growing uneven, his hands trembling slightly at his sides. He was now the one who looked close to a panic attack, his chest rising and falling too quickly as he tried to keep himself together.

    Sherlock, still processing everything John had said, took a cautious step forward. The anger and tension from earlier were gone, replaced by something gentler, steadier. “John,” he said quietly, carefully. “Breathe.”

    John shook his head, his eyes wide, unfocused. “I’ve fucked everything up.” His voice cracked slightly, and he took a step back, his shoulders rigid with tension. “And you’re leaving.”

    “I’m not leaving,” Sherlock said firmly, stepping closer again. He hesitated briefly before placing a hand on John’s arm, grounding him. “I’m still here. It’s alright. And… and I want to be together with you as well.”

    John’s breathing slowed slightly at the contact and his words, though his expression remained tense. “I didn’t think you’d ever… want me,” he admitted quietly, his voice barely above a whisper. “Not after all the times I said I wasn’t… interested in men. I thought you’d assume it meant I didn’t want you at all.”

    Sherlock’s grip on John’s arm tightened slightly, not enough to hurt, but enough to steady him. “I didn’t think you wanted me at all,” he admitted quietly. “Not like that. I thought you wanted something… normal. Someone who could give you what I can’t.”

    John blinked, visibly startled by the admission. “Sherlock…”

    “I don’t expect you to understand everything about me,” Sherlock continued, his tone calm but honest. “I don’t expect anyone to. But what I do expect is for the people I trust to listen when I tell them what I need. And I need you to understand that what happened with Lestrade and Claude - it wasn’t nothing to me. It brought up things I’ve tried very hard to leave behind.”

    John nodded quickly, his earlier defensiveness gone. “I get that now. I do. And I’m so sorry I didn’t before. I should have listened. I should have stopped it.” He hesitated briefly before adding, “But please don’t think I was trying to change you. I wasn’t. I just didn’t realize…” He trailed off, looking frustrated with himself. “I didn’t realize how much it hurt you because I wasn’t thinking about it from your perspective.”

    Sherlock regarded him quietly for a moment, the tension in his chest easing slightly. He could see the sincerity in John’s expression, the genuine regret and concern. Slowly, he let go of John’s arm, though he didn’t move away.

    “I’m not leaving,” he said finally, his voice steady. “But I need you to understand that this can’t happen again. I need to be able to trust that when I say no, you’ll respect it.”

    “You can,” John said quickly, his voice resolute. “I swear, Sherlock. I’ll never do anything like that again.” He exhaled slowly, some of the tension leaving his shoulders. “I just… I didn’t want to lose you.”

    Sherlock’s expression didn’t quite soften, but the tension in his shoulders eased ever so slightly. “You’re not going to lose me,” he said quietly, his voice still a bit rough from everything they had thrown at each other. He hesitated, glancing away for a moment before adding, “And if you’d bothered to ask, you’d have known that I’ve never been interested in anyone else. Just you.”

    John stared at him, clearly stunned. His mouth opened slightly, but no words came out at first. His eyes darted between Sherlock’s, as though trying to process what he had just heard. “Really?” he managed, his voice hoarse, barely above a whisper.

    “Really,” Sherlock said, and though he couldn’t quite smile yet, he started to relax.

    For a few moments, neither of them moved. The exhaustion from the argument hung heavily in the air, the weight of too many emotions leaving them both drained. John’s breathing was still uneven, but slower now, as though he were slowly regaining control. Sherlock stood still, waiting, unsure what to do next but unwilling to leave things unfinished.

    Without warning, John stepped forward, closing the small gap between them, and wrapped his arms around Sherlock in a tight, desperate hug. Sherlock stiffened at first, unaccustomed to the sudden contact, but he didn’t pull away. Slowly, hesitantly, he raised his arms and returned the embrace, his movements awkward but sincere.

    Neither of them spoke for a while. They simply stood there, holding on to each other, the tension that had gripped them all evening finally starting to dissolve into something calmer, something quieter.

    John exhaled a long, shuddering breath against Sherlock’s shoulder. “I was so scared,” he admitted quietly. “I thought I’d ruined everything. I thought you’d leave, and I didn’t know how to fix it.”

    “I wasn’t going to leave,” Sherlock said softly. “But I needed space. Needed time to think.”

    John tightened his grip slightly. “I don’t want you to feel like you can’t trust me anymore. I’ll do better, I swear.”

    Sherlock stayed quiet for a moment, his mind still navigating through the chaos of everything that had happened. His hand rested on John’s back, steady but tentative, as though still wary of holding on too tightly. “I know,” he said eventually, his voice low, quieter now. “Just… don’t decide things for me. Ask.”

    John let out a tired breath, one that seemed to carry the weight of his earlier tension. “Okay,” he murmured, voice still edged with exhaustion but carrying a note of quiet relief. “Okay.”

    After a few more minutes, Sherlock finally pulled away and made his way to the couch, sinking down into it with a sigh. He leaned back, letting the quiet settle around him, though his mind kept turning over what John had said - I love you.

    John followed soon after, dropping onto the other end of the sofa. He sat in silence, hands resting loosely on his knees, his gaze distant. He wasn’t fidgeting, wasn’t looking for something to say. He was simply… present, worn out, as if the fight had drained every bit of energy from him.

    Sherlock glanced at him, his eyes flicking over the familiar lines of John’s face. He wasn’t sure what he’d expected after a confession like that - some sort of dramatic shift, perhaps, or a sudden clarity about what it all meant. Instead, it felt oddly quiet, like standing in the aftermath of a storm, uncertain whether the skies were truly clear or if more rain would come.

    John shifted slightly, his head lolling back against the sofa as he let out a quiet sigh. “We’re okay, right?” he asked softly, his voice barely above a whisper. He seemed so vulnerable that Sherlock’s heart ached. Sherlock hesitated only for a moment before reaching out, his fingers slipping gently into John’s hair. The strands were soft beneath his touch, and he moved his hand in slow, steady strokes, more for John’s comfort than his own. John blinked slowly, the tension in his face easing with each pass of Sherlock’s hand. He slumped over until he was pressed against Sherlock's side.

    “We will be,” Sherlock said quietly, his voice steady but lacking its usual crispness. He too felt completely drained, worn down by the weight of their argument and everything that had come before. But as he bent his arm and continued to stroke John’s hair, he knew that his words were true. It wasn’t a resolution, not an ending, but a starting point. Something they could build on, together. "And John... I love you, too."

    John smiled, eyes closed, murmuring something too quiet to make out, his eyes drifting shut as he finally seemed to relax. His breathing evened out, and within moments, he had fallen asleep, his head resting against Sherlock’s shoulder. For a while, Sherlock stayed there, hand still moving absently through John’s hair, his own mind beginning to settle. The earlier tension still lingered at the edges, but it no longer felt insurmountable. Instead, a quiet sort of hope had begun to take its place, fragile but real. 

    He knew that when he eventually fell asleep there would be no more unpleasant dreams - only the peaceful promise of a better tomorrow.
